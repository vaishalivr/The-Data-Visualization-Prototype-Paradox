As an architect turned designer turned developer, I am often struck by the resonances between the work of a designer and of a developer. Design is all mess and being loose at start. It takes a few iterations to get to understand the problem space and know which affordances to break to bring out something new. The work of a developer around these prototypes is also all mess and being loose at start. But it takes a structured set of logic and rules to make a design team’s prototypes work.
However, in my opinion, over the course of years the trend to make designers think about a problem space is taking a turn towards engineering-centric-way more than being creativity-centered. I say so because when I see these modern design tools are introducing a DOM like layout mechanism - components, variables, auto layouts and more - and while it certainly has its advantages (as seen in Figma Config 2025), it starts to sets limits to all possible design expressions. It is not easy to try odd combinations or paste something into a frame without snapping somewhere to its area. There is an over reliance on the inbuilt grids and auto layout features that tends to reduce the experimental features of design. Creativity also takes a back seat while trying to break symmetry or going off-the-grid on purpose.
Another push to designer-turned-engineering-thinker is the recent Dev Mode which is - in theory - the missing bridge between design specifications and technical implementation. But the mindset of a designer is far far away from the technology that they are designing for.
In order to verify these design ideas - keeping in mind the key aspect that you cannot build a huge product all at once from prototypes - you have to force yourself to view them through the lens of logic and code. And it is during this process, we truly understand the tradeoffs while writing code for interactivity, data loading and test cases.
This often happens when working with data - the missing values, the outliers, the acceptable types and range of values. The easiest work around is to remove them, but then what is left in hand is not the true representation of the original dataset.
Okay, if you cannot get this and that at the same time — what is more important??
Lately, I find myself drifting towards the ides of making a simple prototype using pen, paper or a design tool without a lot of pixel pushing, to be able to get ideas out of my head and then swiftly moving towards executing it in code.
I use these prototypes as a proxy for my ideas and questions that come up during the design phase but testing with code ensures that all critical mistakes are made early on and the bridge between design and development is aligned.
